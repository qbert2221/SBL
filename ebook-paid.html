CHAPTER ONE

Choosing the right corporate structure.

The free guide explains that you need a corporation. This chapter explains which kind, and why the decision is more consequential than most people realize.

There are three corporate structures commonly used for the Corporate Dividend Mortgage™: an LLC taxed as a partnership or disregarded entity, an LLC or corporation electing S-Corp status, or a traditional C-Corp. Each has materially different tax treatment, asset-protection characteristics, and operational complexity. Choosing wrong is recoverable but expensive — every conversion involves paperwork, legal fees, and potentially triggered tax events.

The choice depends on three variables: how much income the structure will generate, whether you intend to hold long-term or sell within ten years, and which state you'll register in. We'll work through each option and provide the framework to make the call for your situation.

§1.1The three structures, side by side

StructureTax treatmentBest forWatch out for
LLC (Default) Pass-through. Profits/losses flow to your personal return as ordinary income. Smaller deals (<$500K home), single-owner setups, those who want simplicity. Self-employment tax on net earnings if you're not careful about passive vs active treatment.
LLC + S-Corp Election Pass-through, but you can take distributions instead of all-salary. Most practitioners. The sweet spot of tax efficiency, asset protection, and operational manageability. Reasonable salary requirement, payroll administration overhead, state-level S-Corp recognition rules.
C-Corp Entity-level taxation at 21%, then dividends taxed again personally. Larger structures (>$1M), those building multi-property portfolios, those planning a corporate sale exit. Double taxation if profits are distributed. Requires more sophisticated bookkeeping and corporate governance.
The recommended default

For most readers, the right answer is an LLC with S-Corp election, registered in your home state if you're holding property there, or in Wyoming/Delaware if the property is elsewhere or if asset-protection is a primary concern. This structure offers the best balance of tax efficiency, simplicity, and flexibility for the typical $200K–$500K total capital deployment that this strategy targets.

§1.2The state-of-formation question

Where you form the entity matters as much as which type. The general rule: register in the state where the property sits. Cross-state structures (e.g., a Wyoming LLC owning California real estate) require foreign qualification in the property's state, which adds annual filing fees, registered agent costs, and complexity.

The exceptions where forming out-of-state is worth it:

  • Wyoming or Delaware when asset protection is a primary concern (charging-order protection is stronger), and you're willing to pay for foreign qualification in the property's state.
  • Nevada for similar privacy/protection reasons, though Nevada's annual fees have risen significantly in recent years.
  • South Dakota or Texas if you live there — both states offer strong corporate frameworks with no state income tax.

For a single-property setup in a tax-friendly state, the local-formation answer is almost always correct. The complexity of multi-state structures rarely pays off below $1M in total assets.

§1.3The decision tree

Which structure should I use?
Are you the only owner?
Yes → Start with an LLC.
Total capital deployed?
< $200K → LLC, default tax treatment. Simple.
$200K – $1M → LLC with S-Corp election. Recommended default.
> $1M → Consider C-Corp, especially if planning multiple properties.
No (multi-owner) → LLC with operating agreement. Allocate via membership interests.
Important caveat

This framework is general guidance based on the typical scenarios this strategy targets. Your specific situation — state of residence, existing income, retirement structures, prior business interests — can materially change the right answer. The final decision must always involve a CPA and an attorney, both familiar with both real estate corporate structures and securities-backed lending. Budget $1,500–$3,000 for proper professional setup; it pays for itself many times over in avoided mistakes.

CHAPTER TWO

The lender shortlist.

Most banks won't touch this structure. The ones that will fall into a few specific categories — and knowing how to talk to them is half the battle.

Walk into a typical retail bank and ask for a mortgage to be issued to a freshly-formed LLC, secured partially by pledged stock — and you'll be met with confusion at best, refusal at worst. The standard residential mortgage product simply isn't built for this structure. You're not looking for retail banks. You're looking for a specific subset of lenders who understand asset-based lending, securities-backed lending (SBL), and corporate-held real estate financing.

These lenders generally fall into four categories. Your shortlist will pull from all four, depending on geography and deal size.

§2.2How to approach a lender (the pitch)

Walking in with the right framing is critical. You don't say "I want a creative financing structure" — that signals risk and complexity. You say "I'm purchasing investment property through a holding entity, with a securities-backed equity contribution". You're describing a normal commercial real estate transaction with one variation: the equity portion is collateralized by liquid securities rather than cash.

Your initial conversation should establish three things in this order:

  1. The property — address, purchase price, property type (residential or commercial held in entity), intended use (rental, primary, hybrid).
  2. The structure — your corporation as borrower, with you as personal guarantor (most lenders will require this, and it's reasonable to accept).
  3. The equity contribution — pledged liquid securities held at [your brokerage], with current market value of $X. You can describe this as "asset-backed equity" or "securities-secured down payment" depending on the lender's vocabulary.
The pitch line that works

"I'm financing an investment property through a corporate holding entity. The down payment is structured as a pledge of marketable securities held at [Brokerage], rather than a cash down payment. Total securities value is approximately [$X], and I'd like to discuss how your asset-based or commercial real estate group might structure this." — This single sentence positions you correctly with sophisticated lenders and tells unsophisticated ones to refer you elsewhere.

§2.3What lenders will require from you

Expect a more thorough underwriting than a residential mortgage, but a less invasive one in some ways. Lenders will want:

  • Personal financial statement (your overall net worth picture)
  • Two years of personal tax returns
  • Brokerage statements showing the pledged securities
  • Corporate formation documents and EIN
  • An appraisal of the property
  • Title commitment
  • Personal guarantee (almost always required)

What they typically won't require, that retail residential lenders would: pay stubs, employment verification letters, debt-to-income calculations based on W-2 income. The qualification basis is shifted from personal income to total assets and the property's economics.

CHAPTER THREE

The brokerage rankings.

Your corporate brokerage account is where the dividend engine lives. The wrong choice costs hundreds of basis points in margin rates — which compounds to tens of thousands over the loan term.

Three things matter when choosing the corporate brokerage that will host the dividend engine: the margin rate they charge, whether they support share pledging to external lenders, and the quality of corporate-account servicing. Most retail brokerages will open a corporate account, but only a handful do all three things competitively.

§3.1Margin rates — the most consequential variable

Your margin rate is the single biggest cost variable in this strategy after the mortgage rate itself. A 100bps difference between two brokerages — say 6.5% vs 7.5% — costs roughly $1,000 per year per $100K borrowed. Over a 30-year loan, that's $30,000+ in additional interest, just from picking the wrong broker.

Interactive Brokers Pro is the default recommendation for most readers. The margin rate advantage (typically 100-250bps lower than Fidelity or Schwab for accounts under $500K) directly translates to higher coverage ratios. The platform is built for sophisticated traders, which means more documentation and a steeper learning curve — but for the lifetime cost of the strategy, the savings are substantial.

§3.2The pledging mechanism

For the down payment shares to function as collateral for your mortgage, they need to be pledged to the lender. This is a tripartite agreement among you (the corporation), the brokerage (custodian), and the lender. Mechanically:

  1. The corporation opens an account at the brokerage and funds it with the down-payment securities.
  2. The lender provides a Control Agreement (typically a Securities Account Control Agreement, or SACA) that the brokerage must execute.
  3. The brokerage flags the pledged shares as restricted — they cannot be sold or transferred without lender consent.
  4. Dividends continue to flow to the corporation; only sale proceeds are restricted.

Not all brokerages execute SACAs, and those that do vary widely in the time and friction involved. Interactive Brokers and Schwab Institutional handle this routinely; many smaller brokerages don't. Confirm SACA support before opening the account — discovering a brokerage won't pledge after you've funded is a costly mistake.

CHAPTER FOUR

Selecting sustainable dividend stocks.

High yields are easy to find. Sustainable yields — ones that will still be paying in five years — require a different selection framework.

The single most catastrophic failure mode for the Corporate Dividend Mortgage™ is a dividend cut. The strategy is engineered around predictable monthly income; if that income gets reduced or eliminated, the coverage ratio collapses, and the corporation has to find money from somewhere to keep servicing the mortgage. Picking dividend stocks for this strategy is therefore a fundamentally different exercise than picking them for general portfolio income.

The framework most amateurs use — "find the highest yield" — is exactly wrong. Yields above 15% are usually warning signs, not opportunities. The right framework prioritizes sustainability over magnitude, with five specific metrics.

§4.1The five sustainability metrics

1. Payout ratio. The percentage of earnings (or, for REITs, FFO) paid out as dividends. Above 90% is dangerous — there's no buffer for a bad quarter. Target: 60-85%. A REIT paying out 130% of FFO is borrowing to pay you; that's not sustainable.

2. Dividend coverage history. How many consecutive years has the company paid the dividend, and how many of those years saw an increase? Look for 10+ years of consistent payment and ideally 5+ years of growth. Companies on the "Dividend Aristocrats" or "Dividend Champions" lists meet this bar.

3. Free cash flow trend. Is the underlying business throwing off enough actual cash to cover the dividend, or is the dividend being funded from debt issuance or asset sales? Look at FCF / Dividend Paid — should be comfortably above 1.0x, ideally 1.3x+.

4. Sector concentration. Don't put all your dividend exposure in one sector. A pure REIT portfolio gets crushed when interest rates spike. A pure energy MLP portfolio gets crushed when commodities fall. Spread across 3-5 sectors: REITs, BDCs, MLPs, traditional dividend stocks, and possibly preferred shares.

5. Margin eligibility. Some brokerages exclude certain stocks from margin (low float, OTC, certain MLPs). Verify each prospective holding is fully marginable at your target leverage before committing capital.

§4.2The yield-trap warning signs

Yield trap red flags

If you see any of these in a prospective holding, treat it as a hard "do not buy" signal: (1) Yield above 15% with payout ratio above 100%. (2) Stock price down 30%+ over the past year (the yield is "high" because the price collapsed, and a dividend cut usually follows). (3) Recent debt covenant violations or amendments. (4) Reverse stock splits in the last 24 months. (5) Special dividends substituting for regular dividends. Each of these patterns has historically preceded dividend eliminations.

§4.3The diversification template

For a typical $200K-$300K total deployment, a defensible structure looks like this:

AllocationSector / TypeTarget yieldRole
30-40%Quality REITs (residential, industrial)4-6%Stable income base, inflation hedge
20-30%BDCs (Business Development Cos)8-11%Yield enhancement, cyclical exposure
15-25%MLPs (midstream energy)6-9%Inflation hedge, tax-advantaged distributions
10-20%Dividend Aristocrats (consumer staples, utilities)3-4%Stability anchor, low correlation
5-10%Preferred shares5-7%Lower volatility, fixed-income substitute

This blend produces a weighted-average portfolio yield in the 5-8% range, which is sustainable, diversified, and generates enough income at 2-3× leverage to cover most mortgage scenarios. The 14.4% yield used in the calculator and free guide is a simplified illustration — in real implementation, blending across sectors typically produces lower per-share yields but dramatically more sustainable income.

"A yield you can count on for ten years is worth twice as much as a yield twice as high that disappears in three."
CHAPTER FIVE

Tax optimization inside the corp.

The corporate wrapper opens deductions and treatments individuals can't access. Most practitioners leave 20-30% of their potential after-tax return on the table by failing to use them.

A corporation is a different tax animal than an individual. It pays for things in pre-tax dollars where individuals pay in after-tax dollars. It can deduct interest, depreciation, and operating expenses against its income. It can also do things individuals categorically cannot — like funding retirement accounts at much higher levels, or claiming property depreciation against active rental income.

This chapter covers the four highest-impact tax strategies available to a Corporate Dividend Mortgage™ structure. None of these is exotic; all of them are routine in commercial real estate. The trick is knowing they're available and structuring from day one to use them.

§5.1Interest deduction stacking

The corporation pays two streams of interest: mortgage interest on the property loan, and margin interest on the brokerage borrowing. Both are deductible against the corporation's income, reducing taxable profit. For a typical structure:

Interest deduction example
Annual mortgage interest (Year 1) ≈ $21,400
Annual margin interest = $7,000
─────────────────────────────
Total deductible interest = $28,400
Annual gross dividends = $43,200
Taxable income (pre-other-deductions) = $14,800

That's a structural reduction of taxable income from $43,200 to $14,800 — a 66% reduction — purely from interest deductions. And we haven't yet applied depreciation, qualified dividend treatment, or any other strategy.

§5.2Property depreciation

If the property is held for investment purposes (which it is, by definition, in a corporate structure), it depreciates on a 27.5-year schedule for residential or 39-year schedule for commercial. For a $450K property with land valued at $90K (a typical 80/20 split), the depreciable basis is $360K, generating annual depreciation of:

Annual depreciation
Depreciable basis = $450,000 − $90,000 land = $360,000
Schedule = 27.5 years (residential)
Annual depreciation deduction = ~$13,090

That $13K of annual depreciation flows against the corporation's income. Combined with interest deductions, it's now common for a properly structured Corporate Dividend Mortgage™ to show near-zero or negative taxable income for the first many years — even while generating real cash flow. This is the same dynamic that has made commercial real estate a tax-favored asset class for decades.

Cost segregation studies — which break out property components into shorter depreciation schedules (5, 7, 15 years) — can accelerate this further. For properties above $500K, a cost-seg study (cost: $3K-$8K) typically pays for itself in the first year through bonus depreciation.

§5.3Qualified dividend treatment

Most U.S. dividend stocks pay qualified dividends, taxed at long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income) rather than ordinary income rates. The corporation receives these as ordinary income, but if you've structured as an S-Corp or pass-through LLC, the qualified character flows through to your personal return.

The exception: REIT distributions are typically not qualified — they're taxed as ordinary income. However, the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act introduced a 20% qualified business income (QBI) deduction on REIT dividends through 2025 (with extension under discussion). Structure carefully to maximize qualified dividends in the leveraged block, and treat REIT distributions as the unleveraged stable-yield component.

§5.4Solo 401(k) contributions from corporate income

The least-known but highest-impact strategy: an LLC-S-Corp structure can sponsor a Solo 401(k), into which the owner can contribute meaningful pre-tax amounts as both employee and employer. For 2026, the combined limit is approximately $70,000+ (subject to compensation rules). This means the corporation can functionally convert taxable dividend income into tax-deferred retirement savings — at scales individuals are categorically locked out of.

The combined effect

A practitioner using all four strategies — interest stacking, depreciation, QBI deduction on REIT dividends, and Solo 401(k) — can routinely achieve effective tax rates of 5-15% on the dividend income generated by the structure, versus 22-37% if the same income were earned in a personal name and held outside any tax-advantaged vehicle. Over a 30-year holding period, this difference compounds into hundreds of thousands of dollars.

CHAPTER SIX

The corporate exit advantage.

When you eventually sell, you have an exit route most homeowners don't: you can sell the corporation rather than the house. This changes everything for both you and the buyer.

In a traditional real estate transaction, you sell the property: deed transfers from your name to the buyer's name, you owe capital gains tax on the appreciation, the buyer pays transfer taxes and gets a fresh mortgage at current rates. Standard. Inflexible. Often expensive on both sides.

When you own the property inside a corporation, you have a second option: sell the corporation itself. The buyer acquires the entity, which already owns the home (and any retained portfolio, if structured to include it). The deed never changes hands. The mortgage isn't refinanced. And the tax treatment for both parties can be dramatically more favorable.

§6.1Why a corporate sale can be better for the buyer

Consider what the buyer would normally face: a new mortgage at current market rates, which could be 1-3 percentage points above your original locked-in rate. On a $450K loan, every additional percent of rate is roughly $300/mo more in payments — $108,000 more over 30 years.

If the buyer instead acquires your corporation, they inherit the existing mortgage at its locked-in rate. They get your 5.98% loan instead of today's 7.5% loan. For the buyer, that's tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars in present-value savings — and it's a benefit you can capture as a higher sale price, since you're delivering something materially more valuable than just the property.

"When you own the corporation, you don't sell a house — you sell a financing structure that already happens to include a house."

§6.2Why it can be better for you

For the seller, three advantages stand out. First, transfer taxes: in many states, real property transfers trigger transfer taxes (0.5%–2.5% of value); corporate stock transfers often don't. Second, capital gains treatment: selling the corporation can be structured as a long-term capital gains event on the equity, rather than a property sale subject to depreciation recapture (which would otherwise tax back the depreciation benefits we discussed in Chapter 5 at ordinary income rates). Third, retained portfolio handling: if you've built a substantial dividend portfolio inside the corp over the years, selling it as part of the corporate sale can defer or restructure the capital gains on those positions.

§6.3Structuring from day one for the corporate sale

The corporate-sale option only exists cleanly if you've structured for it from the beginning. A few decisions made on day one matter enormously seven or fifteen years later:

  • Keep the corporation clean — single purpose, single property, single brokerage account. Don't commingle with other business activities. The simpler the entity, the easier the eventual sale.
  • Document everything as if for due diligence — properly maintained corporate minutes, clear separation of personal and corporate expenses, professional bookkeeping from year one. A buyer's attorney will scrutinize all of it.
  • Choose a structure that supports stock sale — C-Corp stock is most cleanly transferable; LLC interests work but require operating-agreement assignability provisions; pure sole-prop structures aren't transferable as entities at all.
  • Consider a holding company layer — for larger setups, putting the operating LLC under a parent C-Corp can simplify both ongoing tax planning and eventual sale.
Important caveat

The corporate sale exit is a powerful option, but it requires both a willing and sophisticated buyer, and either an experienced real estate attorney or M&A counsel to structure properly. Not every property and buyer combination supports it. Treat it as an option you can offer, not a guarantee. A traditional property sale always remains available as the fallback. The point of structuring for it from day one is to keep both doors open, not to commit to one path.

CHAPTER SEVEN

Risk management protocols.

The strategy is robust, but it has specific failure modes. The professionals who use this approach for decades follow a small set of protocols religiously.

Every leveraged strategy has a way to break. The Corporate Dividend Mortgage™ has three primary failure modes — dividend cuts, margin calls, and spread inversion (where margin cost exceeds dividend yield). Each is manageable if you've structured for it and watch the right indicators. Each is catastrophic if you haven't.

This chapter is the operational manual for keeping the structure healthy across decades, including a stress-test framework, the monthly metrics to watch, and the decision triggers for when to act.

§7.1The stress-test framework

Before launching the structure, run it through three stress scenarios. The structure should remain solvent (coverage above 80%) under each:

ScenarioVariable changedTest thresholdWhat to verify
Rate shockMargin rate +200bpsCoverage stays ≥ 100%Spread remains positive
Dividend cutYield reduced by 25%Coverage stays ≥ 80%Personal income can bridge gap
Stock declinePledged shares −30%No margin call triggeredMaintenance margin still satisfied

Run all three simultaneously for the worst-case test. If your structure survives a 200bps rate shock + 25% dividend cut + 30% stock decline with positive coverage and no margin call, you have a robust setup. If it doesn't, deleverage until it does — typically by reducing the leverage multiplier from 2× to 1.5× or 1.25×.

§7.2The monthly dashboard

Every month, check the same five metrics. Anything trending the wrong way is your early warning system:

  1. Coverage ratio — your single most important number. If it drops below 110%, investigate why and remediate.
  2. Margin equity ratio — pledged stock value vs. borrowed amount. Should remain comfortably above the brokerage's maintenance margin (typically 30%, though this varies).
  3. Dividend payment confirmations — verify each expected distribution actually arrived. A missed payment is the first sign of a coming cut.
  4. Margin rate notifications — most brokerages adjust margin rates monthly. Track the trend; rising rates compress your spread.
  5. Cash reserve in corp account — should be at least 3 months of mortgage + margin payments at all times. This is your buffer against late dividends, surprise expenses, or short-term volatility.

§7.3Decision triggers

Pre-commit to specific actions at specific thresholds, made in advance when you're calm. Decisions made under stress are usually wrong:

The pre-committed action plan

  1. If coverage drops below 110% → review and identify cause; reduce discretionary corp spending.
  2. If coverage drops below 100% → contribute personal capital to corp reserves; consider reducing leverage.
  3. If any pledged stock drops 20% from purchase → review thesis; consider replacing with equivalent yield in different name.
  4. If margin rate rises above dividend yield → immediately reduce leverage to 1×; the spread is now negative.
  5. If brokerage issues margin call notification → respond same-day with cash deposit; never let it go to forced liquidation.
  6. If any dividend is cut by > 20% → rotate that holding within 30 days; do not wait for further cuts.
  7. If coverage drops below 80% for two consecutive months → trigger formal restructuring; deleverage substantially.

§7.4The cash reserve discipline

The single discipline that separates practitioners who succeed for decades from those who blow up in the first downturn: maintain a corporate cash reserve equal to 3-6 months of total payments at all times. This is not optional. This is not "nice to have." This is the buffer that absorbs late dividends, unexpected expenses, brief margin compression, and the dozen small surprises that any 30-year structure will encounter.

Fund the reserve from monthly surplus until it's at target, then route additional surplus into either principal paydown, reserve expansion, or new positions. Never deplete the reserve below target without an immediate plan to refill.

CHAPTER EIGHT

The implementation checklist.

Everything you've read, sequenced into the order you actually execute it. Print this chapter. Work it.

The hardest part of implementing a strategy like this is sequencing — knowing what comes first, what depends on what, and which decisions you can postpone versus which must be locked in early. The checklist below is the order experienced practitioners follow. Allow 8 to 16 weeks from start to closing on the property, depending on lender response time and your own pace.

Phase 1: Foundations (Weeks 1-3)

  1. Run your scenario through the calculator. Confirm coverage ratio above 120% under your base assumptions, and above 80% under stress (Chapter 7).
  2. Engage a CPA familiar with corporate real estate structures. Get the corporate-form recommendation in writing.
  3. Engage an attorney to draft formation documents and operating agreement. Budget $1,500-$3,000 for this stage.
  4. File state formation paperwork. Obtain EIN from IRS (free, takes 1 business day).
  5. Open corporate bank account in the entity's name.

Phase 2: Capital Setup (Weeks 3-6)

  1. Open corporate brokerage account at chosen broker (Chapter 3). Confirm SACA support.
  2. Fund the brokerage account with the down payment capital.
  3. Build the dividend portfolio per the diversification template (Chapter 4).
  4. Confirm initial dividend payments arriving as expected. Validate yield calculations against actual receipts.
  5. Build the cash reserve to 3-6 months of expected payments before closing on property.

Phase 3: Property and Financing (Weeks 4-12)

  1. Identify target property. Negotiate purchase contract in the corporation's name (or assignable, then assign).
  2. Approach lenders on the shortlist (Chapter 2). Use the pitch framework. Aim for 3-5 simultaneous conversations.
  3. Submit formal applications to top 2 lenders. Provide all requested documentation promptly.
  4. Coordinate with brokerage on Securities Account Control Agreement (SACA) execution.
  5. Order property appraisal, title commitment, inspections.
  6. Lock interest rate when terms are favorable; finalize loan documents.

Phase 4: Closing and Activation (Weeks 10-16)

  1. Closing: deed transfers to the corporation; mortgage is recorded; SACA is filed.
  2. Set up automatic mortgage payment from corporate account.
  3. Set up automatic margin interest accrual tracking.
  4. Set up monthly dashboard (Chapter 7). Calendar reminder for the first monthly review.
  5. Confirm first month of operations: dividends arrive, margin interest debited, mortgage paid, cash reserve maintained.

Ongoing: Months 2 through 360

  1. Monthly dashboard review. Five metrics. Five minutes. (Chapter 7)
  2. Quarterly: rebalance portfolio if any position has drifted significantly from target.
  3. Annual: full strategic review. Tax filings. Stress-test rerun. Decision: deploy surplus into principal paydown, second property, or portfolio expansion.
  4. Year 5+: begin considering exit framework (Chapter 6) so the corporate sale option remains viable.
Final note from the playbook

This structure is not a get-rich scheme; it's an asset-aware approach to a normal financial transaction. The hard work is in the setup. Once running, the system requires modest ongoing attention — typically 30 minutes a month and a few hours each year. The reward is a 30-year financial structure that pays for itself, builds equity in two assets simultaneously, and never depends on your ability to keep showing up at a job. That's the prize. The playbook is just the route to get there.
